The Police License-Plate-Reader Market

Nobody owns the plate-reader market — yet.

Automated license-plate readers are the fastest-spreading surveillance tool in American policing, and the conventional wisdom is that one vendor already runs the table. We analyzed every public-safety solicitation we could find. The verdict: 92% are open, competitive bids, no-bid lock-ins are falling, and the category is wide open for the taking.

01 — THE ASSUMPTION

One company is supposed to own this category. Does it?

Ask anyone in public safety who sells plate readers and you'll hear the same name. The category leader has become shorthand for the entire product — the way "Kleenex" stands in for tissues. The assumption that follows is fatal for a sales team: that the market is already locked up, and there's no point bidding.

So we tested it. Using NationGraph's index of government solicitations, we pulled every license-plate-reader RFP issued by a fire, EMS, or police-sector buyer between January 2024 and June 2026 — then stripped out duplicates, parking-enforcement systems, and non-public-safety agencies. What remained was a clean picture of real law-enforcement demand: 167 solicitations from at least 82 agencies in 29 states. Here is what that demand actually looks like.

The data tells a different story than the reputation does.

03 — THE TREND

If the leader were closing the market, no-bids would be rising. They're falling.

Sole-source share of all public-safety plate-reader solicitations, by year. The lock-out rate peaked in 2025 and then dropped by more than half — even as total volume stayed high.

The takeaway for a challenger: the window isn't closing. In the first half of 2026, just 2 of 48 solicitations were sole-source. Agencies are increasingly writing open, vendor-neutral specs — and inviting exactly the competition you'd bring.

06 — THE SHAPE OF THE MARKET

This isn't a big-city story. It's small-town America.

Where a brand is named, Flock Safety appears most — and its edge shows up as sole-source awards (Haines City, FL: "Flock Safety ALPR Cameras, Sole Source"), where an agency is convinced nothing comparable exists.

The counter is already in the data: Burke County, GA wrote its spec for cameras "comparable to Flock Safety technology" — an agency openly inviting a challenger. Those "comparable-to" RFPs are your highest-fit targets.

PLAYBOOK

Four moves for a vendor selling into public safety.

01

Sell to the city, not the org chart

Target municipal and county procurement on behalf of PDs and sheriffs — ~90% of the buying authority. Type-based prospect filters miss it entirely.

02

Skip the 8%, win the 92%

Sole-source notices are dead ends — don't spend the cycle. Pour effort into open, vendor-neutral solicitations where you can still shape the spec.

03

Hunt the "comparable-to" RFPs

When an agency specs "comparable to Flock," it is explicitly inviting a challenger. These are the highest-conversion targets in the dataset.

04

Arrive before the RFP drops

An ALPR solicitation flags an agency whose budget and tech appetite are both live — and a chance to influence requirements before the bid is even written.

A plate-reader RFP is a modernization tripwire

Agencies issuing ALPR solicitations are frequently in-market for adjacent technology at the same time. The same dataset surfaces body-worn and in-car camera replacements and police drone / UAS buys from the same tier of department. A plate-reader bid is a reliable indicator that an agency's budget and appetite for technology are both live — useful whether you sell cameras, evidence management, fleet telematics, or real-time crime-center software.

Selling to fire or EMS? Same signal, your category.

This brief profiles plate readers because that's where law-enforcement demand is loudest right now. The method — counting named agencies with an open, dated buying need — works identically for fire and EMS: SCBA and turnout-gear replacements, ambulance remounts, fire-apparatus RFPs, and AFG / SAFER grant-funded equipment cycles. The signal is there; it just needs to be cut for your product.
