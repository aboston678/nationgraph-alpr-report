Open Season on the Plate-Reader Market

Since January 2024, at least 81 police departments, sheriff's offices, and state law-enforcement agencies across 29 states have put automated license-plate readers out to bid. Just 8% were written as sole-source, no-bid awards — the other 92% are open, competitive solicitations. The fastest-spreading surveillance technology in American policing is, by the numbers, a wide-open market.

The Signal

If you sell to law enforcement, this is one of the most active line items in the country.

Automated license-plate readers (ALPR) — the fixed and mobile cameras that photograph every passing vehicle, log its plate, and store where and when it was seen — have become a default purchase for American policing. NationGraph identified at least 167 distinct plate-reader solicitations from fire/EMS/police-sector buyers between January 2024 and June 2026.

That figure is deliberately conservative. It counts unique solicitations after collapsing duplicate and reposted listings, and it excludes parking-enforcement systems, housing authorities, transit, and schools — leaving only genuine public-safety demand. Even after that filtering, roughly four dozen additional matching records couldn't be tied to a named agency, so every number in this brief is a floor, not a ceiling. The real market is larger.

Why this matters for targeting

Only 8 of every ~120 resolved buyers are filed under a formal "police agency" record. If your prospecting list filters to law-enforcement entities by type, you are missing roughly 90% of the market.

The purchase order originates with the city or the county. State demand is real too — Maryland State Police, Pennsylvania State Police, the Texas DPS / Rangers, the South Carolina Law Enforcement Division, and DC's Metropolitan Police all appear in the data.

The market leader is visible — but it isn't locking the market down.

This is the heart of the brief. A category that everyone assumes is "owned" by one fast-growing vendor is, in procurement terms, overwhelmingly open. Fewer than one in twelve agencies wrote their solicitation to exclude competition. For the other 92%, the technical specification, the evaluation criteria, and the award are all still contestable.

Read the incumbent's playbook

Where a brand is named at all, Flock Safety appears most — ahead of Motorola's Vigilant line, Rekor, and Genetec. Its edge shows up as sole-source awards (e.g., Haines City, FL — "Flock Safety ALPR Cameras, Sole Source"), where an agency is convinced nothing comparable exists.

The counter is already in the data: Burke County, GA wrote its spec for cameras "comparable to Flock Safety technology" — an agency openly inviting a challenger. Those "comparable-to" solicitations are your highest-fit targets.

What To Do With This

Four moves for a vendor selling into public safety.

01

Sell to the city, not the org chart

Target municipal and county procurement on behalf of PDs and sheriffs — that's ~90% of the buying authority. Type-based prospect filters miss it.

02

Skip the 8%, win the 92%

Sole-source notices are dead ends — don't spend the cycle. Concentrate effort on open, vendor-neutral solicitations where you can still shape the spec.

03

Hunt the "comparable-to" RFPs

When an agency writes its spec as "comparable to Flock Safety," it is explicitly inviting a challenger. These are your highest-conversion targets.

04

Get there before the RFP drops

An ALPR solicitation signals an agency whose budget and tech appetite are both active — and a chance to influence requirements before the bid is even written.

A plate-reader RFP is a modernization tripwire

Agencies issuing ALPR solicitations are frequently in-market for adjacent technology at the same time. The same dataset surfaces body-worn and in-car camera replacements and police drone / UAS buys from the same tier of department. A plate-reader bid is a reliable indicator that an agency's budget and appetite for technology are both live — useful whether you sell cameras, evidence management, fleet telematics, or real-time crime-center software.

Selling to fire or EMS? The same signal exists for you.

This brief profiles plate readers because that's where law-enforcement demand is loudest right now. But the method — counting named agencies with an open, dated buying need — works identically for fire and EMS: SCBA and turnout-gear replacements, ambulance remounts, fire-apparatus RFPs, and AFG / SAFER grant-funded equipment cycles. The procurement signal is there; it simply needs to be cut for your category.
