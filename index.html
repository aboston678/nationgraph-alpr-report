THE PROBLEM

You're prospecting at the end of the buying motion.

Most teams find public-safety deals the same way: a bid alert fires, a purchase order shows up, or they work a list of known accounts filtered by "police department." All three are late. By the time an RFP posts, requirements are often written, a favored vendor may already be shaping the spec, and the contestable window is closing.

The opportunity isn't hidden because agencies aren't buying. It's hidden because the buying signal is scattered across the wrong records — before the RFP, under the wrong buyer name. This brief follows the breadcrumbs of one fast-moving category, license-plate readers, to show where the motion actually lives.

FACT 1 — THE RFP IS LATE

Buying intent shows up first in meetings, budgets, grants, and approvals.

Across the set, the earliest public sign of an ALPR purchase is almost never the RFP. It's a council ALPR presentation (North Port, 2020), a sole-source authorization (Coeur d'Alene, 2024), a capital-budget line (Mequon, WI's Flock expansion, 2023), or a federal grant earmarked to "procure LPRs" (Ormond Beach, FL — JAG, 2023). The RFP is where the motion becomes obvious — and crowded.

What a bid alert misses

An RFP alert tells you the spec exists. It can't tell you the requirements were shaped in a committee meeting months earlier — or that a sole-source letter is already being drafted.

FACT 2 — THE BUYER IS HIDDEN

The end user is police. The buyer of record is the city.

Of 119 named buyers, the purchasing authority sits with a municipal office the majority of the time — buying on behalf of a police department, not as a standalone law-enforcement entity. ~65% post under a municipal record; add county and state procurement offices and most buying authority sits outside a police record entirely.

FACT 3 — THE DEMAND IS CONTESTABLE

Open competitive bids show vendors can still win — if they arrive early.

Of 167 solicitations, 154 were open competitive bids; 13 were sole-source. Open competitive means the agency is formally inviting responses — the path isn't closed. One honest caveat, kept short: "open" and "winnable" aren't the same. Some open RFPs are written around an incumbent before they post; we can't see pre-wiring from the public record. What we can say: most visible demand isn't formally locked to a vendor when it appears.

FACT 4 — TIMING DECIDES WINNABILITY

Open after the RFP is not the same as influence before it.

The single most important sales line in this brief: the challenger's window is open until the sole-source letter gets written. Reach the agency while it's still benchmarking — drafting requirements, comparing vendors, debating in committee — and an open RFP is a real opening. Show up when the RFP posts and you may be reading a decision that was made in stages 2–4. The data point that proves the stakes: North Port's "solicitation" was already a sole-source award notice. The early signals were the only way in.

FACT 5 — THE LONG TAIL IS SHAPEABLE

The volume is small-town America — where specs aren't hardened yet.

Among municipal buyers with population on record (n = 48), the median ALPR buyer serves about 53,000 people; two-thirds serve under 100,000. Smaller agencies have thinner technical staff and less vendor-evaluation muscle — more room to educate, frame tradeoffs, and shape requirements before they harden.

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR VENDORS

Stop waiting for the RFP alert.

01

Prospect by procurement authority

Map the buyer of record (city / county / finance / board), not just the police department. That alone recovers the market you can't currently see.

02

Monitor pre-RFP signals

Watch council, committee, budget, and grant records — the stages where requirements are still being defined.

03

Arrive before the spec hardens

Prioritize open, vendor-neutral demand and "comparable-to" specs — and reach the agency while it's still benchmarking.

04

Treat ALPR as a modernization tripwire

A plate-reader signal often rides with RTCC software, camera networks, evidence management, CAD/RMS, body cameras, and drones.
