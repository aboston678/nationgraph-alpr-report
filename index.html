THE THESIS — ONE CLAIM, THREE FACTS

Most of the police plate-reader market is bid out in the open, to buyers most vendors never see — so the opening is to reach the hidden, contestable majority first.

What this brief is NOT claiming

We're not saying Flock is losing or the category is wide open. Flock has a real moat, and many of its wins close before an RFP ever appears — pilots, council approvals, cooperative contracts — so this RFP-based view undercounts the leader. The claim is narrower and more useful: alongside those quiet wins sits a large, named, contestable slice of demand, and most teams never see it. The three facts below are the map of that slice.

FACT 01 · WHO — THE BLINDSPOT

Your customer is a city or a county — not an "agency" on a list.

The single most expensive assumption in public-safety prospecting is that you find police buyers by filtering for police agencies. You don't. Of 119 named buyers, the purchase authority sits with a city or county procurement office the overwhelming majority of the time — buying on behalf of a department, not as a standalone law-enforcement entity.

FACT 03 · HOW — OPEN & CONTESTABLE

Most of this demand is bid in the open.

Of the 167 solicitations, 154 are open, competitive bids and just 13 are sole-source. In 89% of specs, no vendor is named at all.

Read it honestly: competitive bidding is legally required above most dollar thresholds, so an open RFP is the default posture, not proof the incumbent loses. What "open" buys you is narrower and still valuable — the technical spec, the evaluation criteria, and the shortlist are all still in play, if you engage before the bid publishes. A sole-source notice is the opposite: the vendor's pre-selected and the cycle's a dead end. Skip those.

Where a brand does appear, the leader dominates — and shows up as sole-source awards (Haines City, FL: "Flock Safety ALPR Cameras, Sole Source"). Your sharpest targets are the "comparable-to" specs: when Burke County, GA writes for cameras "comparable to Flock Safety technology," it's openly benchmarking alternatives — the clearest shot a challenger gets.

One honest caveat: "comparable to Flock" isn't a guaranteed opening — it can signal an agency that wants Flock-grade capability and still lands on Flock. It's where your odds are best, not where they're certain.

Sole-source share has stayed in single digits every year — but with this sample we won't dress a few data points up as a trend. The defensible read: lock-outs are the exception, not the rule.

THE PLAYBOOK

What to do about it — four moves.

01 · FROM FACT 1

Prospect by procurement office, not the org chart

~90% of buying authority sits with city and county procurement acting for PDs and sheriffs. Type-based filters that hunt for "law-enforcement agencies" miss most of the market. Build your list around the buyer of record.

02 · FROM FACT 2

Work the long tail

The median buyer is a city of 53,000, and two-thirds are under 100k. That's where the incumbent's network advantage is weakest and references compound fastest. Hundreds of small agencies beat a dozen flagship cities.

03 · FROM FACT 3

Get into open specs early

Vendor-neutral and "comparable to Flock" solicitations are where a challenger can still shape requirements. The leverage is in timing: influence the spec while it's being written, not after it's published.

04 · THE BONUS

Treat an ALPR RFP as a modernization tripwire

An agency issuing a plate-reader solicitation has budget and tech appetite live right now — and is frequently in-market for adjacent systems (body cameras, drones, RTCC) at the same time.

A plate-reader RFP is a modernization tripwire

Agencies issuing ALPR solicitations are often buying adjacent technology in the same cycle. The same dataset surfaces body-worn and in-car camera replacements and police drone / UAS purchases from the same tier of department — useful whether you sell cameras, evidence management, fleet telematics, or real-time crime-center software.

Selling to fire or EMS? Same signal, your category.

This brief profiles plate readers because that's where law-enforcement demand is loudest right now. The method — counting named agencies with an open, dated buying need — works identically for fire and EMS: SCBA and turnout-gear replacements, ambulance remounts, fire-apparatus RFPs, and AFG / SAFER grant cycles.

How we counted — so you can trust the pipeline

  • ✓Public-safety buyers only. Parking enforcement, housing authorities, transit, sanitation, schools, and universities were filtered out — fire / EMS / police demand exclusively.
  • ✓Counts, not dollars. We measure how many agencies are soliciting — the cleanest demand signal. Underlying dollar fields are unreliable and were not used.
  • ✓Deduplicated and conservative. Reposted and "updated" listings were collapsed by normalized source URL; ~48 records couldn't be resolved to a named agency, so every figure is a floor.
  • ✓Intent, not awards. These are solicitations — how deals are opened and structured, not who ultimately won them. Every named agency traces to a specific RFP and source URL.
  • ✓Visible demand only. Deals the incumbent closes before an RFP — pilots, direct approvals, cooperative contracts — fall outside the dataset. This is a floor on contestable demand, not total market share.
