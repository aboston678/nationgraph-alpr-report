THE CLAIM

By the time you see the bid, the deal is usually already shaped.

Every vendor selling to public safety watches the same thing: posted RFPs, purchase orders, spend history. It feels like the front of the funnel. It's the back. A license-plate-reader purchase moves through council discussions, capital budgets, grant awards, vendor demos, and sole-source letters long before a solicitation hits a bid database — and those records are public the whole time.

So we tested it the only honest way: by walking real agencies end to end, from their first public ALPR signal to the day the bid finally appeared. Two of them below — then the pattern behind them.

The bid isn't the starting gun. It's the eulogy.

THE PATTERN — NOT TWO ANECDOTES

The buying motion has six stages. Bid tools wake up at stage five.

North Port and East Ridge aren't outliers — they're the shape of the category. The decision forms across the first four stages; the RFP and the award are the last two.

"But most bids are open" — true, and worth meeting head-on. Of 167 solicitations, 154 post as open competitive bids and only 13 say "sole-source" out loud. But open is a legal posture, not an open contest. By the time an open ALPR bid posts, the capability is routinely already funded and the spec already shaped — North Port's "open" program was a sole-source award by the time it surfaced, and the 13 are only the deals that admit it. WHERE THE SIGNAL LIVES

The decision is on the public record — just not under "police."

The buying motion isn't hidden; it's fragmented. The records that reveal it — capital budgets, council and committee votes, grant awards, sole-source letters — sit with city, county, and finance offices, not police departments. Of 119 named buyers in the proof set, roughly 65% post under a municipal record.

WHAT THE PATTERN IMPLIES

Reading the market by RFP understates it — and arrives late.

01

The market is larger than bid feeds show

With ~65% of buyers filing under municipal rather than police records, a police-department or bid-portal view systematically undercounts the active buyer pool.

02

Requirements are set before the solicitation

Stages 1–4 — budget, committee, benchmark language, sole-source justification — fix the specification. A posted RFP records a decision that is largely already made.

03

"Open" overstates contestability

154 of 167 bids are open competitive, but openness is a legal posture, not proof a deal is winnable — as North Port's open program turned sole-source illustrates.

04

The buy is funded before it's bid

70+ agencies across 24 states bought ALPR with federal grants — Byrne/JAG, Homeland Security, congressional earmarks. Each is a dated, public commitment to a specific system, recorded where no bid portal looks.

Verdict · the RFP is the last signal.

In the public-safety license-plate-reader market, the buying decision forms upstream — in budgets, council votes, grants, and sole-source letters — and is frequently settled before a solicitation reaches a bid portal. North Port's bid surfaced as a sole-source award after five years of public signals; East Ridge's ALPR program was grant-funded and settled in open council, while the only RFP a bid feed ever saw was for the electrical service to power the cameras. The RFP documents the decision — or misses it entirely; it does not open it.

That is not the same as saying every deal is closed. 154 of 167 solicitations post as open, competitive bids — but "open" is a posture, not a guarantee, and the contestable window narrows as the specification hardens across stages one through four. The signal that decides the outcome is the one before the RFP, and it sits in records most market tools never read.
