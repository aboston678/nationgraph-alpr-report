THE FINDING

NationGraph analyzed every public-safety ALPR solicitation from January 2024 through June 2026: 167 buying notices, 82 named agencies, 29 states. Real RFPs, bids, and quotes — not market-size estimates, not inferred intent.

Here is the finding most vendors miss: roughly 90% of those buyers were not filed under a formal "police agency" record. The cop shop uses the camera. The City of X purchasing office posts the bid. So a sales team that prospects by "police department" — which is almost all of them — is structurally blind to most of its own pipeline.

That's not a market-size problem. It's a lookup problem. And lookup problems are fixable.

WHY IT BEATS "FLOCK ALREADY WON"

There's a second market underneath the visible one.

The category story you've heard — Flock dominates, the network effect compounds, deals close before a bid goes public — is true. But it describes the visible market everyone's already fighting over. The public record shows a second market beneath it: agencies entering procurement through city, county, and finance channels, recurring, spread across 29 states, and mostly contestable.

FACT 1 — THE BUYER IS HIDDEN

The buyer is filed under the wrong record.

In most cases the buyer of record is not a standalone PD. The purchase posts through a city, county, procurement department, finance office, or board of commissioners acting on behalf of law enforcement. End user: police. Budget justification: public safety. Record on file: "City of X — General Services."

FACT 2 — THE DEMAND IS OPEN

Most of the demand is genuinely contestable.

Of 167 solicitations, 154 were open competitive bids; only 13 were sole-source. Sole-source means the buyer has effectively picked someone — low-probability for a challenger. Open competitive means the agency is formally inviting responses. The path isn't closed.

One honest caveat, up front so it isn't a gotcha: "open" and "winnable" are not the same thing. Some open RFPs are written around an incumbent before they're ever posted, and we can't see pre-wiring from the public record. What we can say precisely: most visible ALPR demand is not formally locked to a vendor at the moment it appears. That's the opening — whether you walk through it depends on how early you get there.

FACT 3 — THE OPPORTUNITY IS IN THE LONG TAIL

This isn't the big cities. It's the long tail.

Among municipal buyers with population data (n = 48), the median ALPR buyer served about 53,000 people; two-thirds served fewer than 100,000. Small jurisdictions have thinner technical staff and less vendor-evaluation muscle — so a challenger can still educate, frame tradeoffs, and shape requirements before they harden. And references travel: one win propagates through sheriff networks and cooperative purchasing groups to the agencies next door.

WHAT TO ACTUALLY DO

Four moves — each mapped to a finding.

FROM FACT 1

Prospect by procurement authority, not department name

Map both the operational agency and the buyer of record — city, county, finance office, board of commissioners. This alone recovers most of the pipeline you're blind to.

FROM FACT 2

Filter for open + vendor-neutral; deprioritize sole-source

13 of 167 are near-dead on arrival. Don't spend equally across all 167 — concentrate where the agency is formally inviting responses.

FROM FACT 2 (caveat)

Get in before the spec hardens

Once it's posted, requirements may already be shaped. The window is when the agency is researching, drafting, and budgeting — pre-RFP.

FROM FACT 3

Work the sub-100k market deliberately

It's the largest, least-mature, most-reachable slice of visible demand — and references compound through regional networks.

One extension: an ALPR bid is rarely a single-product signal. The same agency is often modernizing RTCC software, camera networks, evidence management, CAD/RMS, body cameras, or drones. ALPR is a tripwire for a broader public-safety tech refresh.

WHAT THIS DOESN'T CLAIM

The incumbent isn't weak. Not every open bid is winnable. And 167 is a floor, not the whole market — pilots, co-op contracts, council approvals, and renewals never surface as competitive solicitations. The point isn't that we've sized the market. It's that the visible, contestable floor is already big enough to build a pipeline on — if you're searching the right records.

The ALPR market isn't hidden because agencies aren't buying. It's hidden because the buying signal is attached to the wrong record.

The vendors who win surface those signals first, sort the contestable from the pre-wired, and reach the buyer before the requirements set. That's not a prospecting-effort problem — it's a data problem, and data problems have answers.
